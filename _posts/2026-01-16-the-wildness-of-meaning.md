Today’s poem was [**Wild Geese**](https://allpoetry.com/poem/15374223-Wild-geese-by-Mary-J-Oliver) by Mary Oliver, and it felt immediately as though it belonged in the same ongoing conversation as the other poems I’ve been reading.

I’m not sure whether this says something fundamental about poetry itself—that a large proportion of poems are inevitably drawn toward questions of meaning, nature, and perception—or whether it reflects a bias in what ChatGPT tends to surface, particularly from American poetry. Maybe I’ll deliberately ask for something very different next time. But that doesn’t take away from how much I enjoyed *Wild Geese*. I enjoyed it immensely.

For me, the poem felt like a meeting point between two earlier strands. On the one hand, it carries something recognisably **Wordsworthian**: the sense that nature, perceived rightly, holds transcendence within it. On the other hand, it shares a directness and clarity that reminded me of [William Carlos Williams]({% post_url 2026-01-08-the-consolation-and-limits-of-immanence %})—a refusal to over-intellectualise, a grounding of meaning in lived experience rather than abstraction.

But Oliver is also doing something distinct, and more explicit.

## The Rejection of Moral Scaffolding

The poem opens with a striking rejection:

> You do not have to be good.
> You do not have to walk on your knees
> for a hundred miles through the desert, repenting.

This feels recognisably Wordsworthian in its underlying intuition: that transcendence has not disappeared so much as migrated, and that it remains available in the natural world.

But unlike Wordsworth, who tends to approach this surplus of meaning with reverence and delicacy, Oliver is strikingly direct—almost coarse—in how she clears space for it. Her rejection is not gradual or suggestive; it is blunt. You do not have to be good. You do not have to suffer correctly.

The **moral scaffolding** is kicked away at the outset. Where Wordsworth gently reorients the reader toward nature as a site of renewal, Oliver forcefully dismisses what she takes to be a false gatekeeper to meaning, replacing it not with doctrine or argument, but with an unapologetic insistence on immediacy and permission.

## Biological Allowance

What she offers instead is not instruction, but **allowance**:

> You only have to let the soft animal of your body
> love what it loves.

Here, meaning is grounded in the body—but not in the domestic, object-centred way it is in Williams. Williams’ meaning often lives in tools, routines, places: wheelbarrows, kitchens, plums. Oliver’s meaning feels more biological and affective. It’s not something you *attend* to so much as something you *allow*. You don’t cultivate it; you get out of its way.

There is a generosity in this move, but also a confidence. Meaning is not difficult. It’s not something you earn. It’s not even something you discover. It’s something you already carry.

## The Fragility of the Domestic

One of the reservations I’ve found myself developing about William Carlos Williams is that, although his meaning is immanent and effortless, it also feels fragile. It seems to rely on a kind of childlike consciousness—on the availability of positive memories, familiar routines, and a world that has already felt safe and coherent.

The red wheelbarrow and the plums in the icebox are saturated with meaning, but that saturation depends on a history of care and belonging. Williams’ meaning works beautifully when one can lean on such memories, but it feels less clear how it sustains itself once those conditions are lost—through age, suffering, or rupture.

What it largely lacks is **Transcendence**: a sense of meaning that exceeds the individual’s personal history and continues even when attention, memory, or affection falter.

*Wild Geese* can be read, in part, as a response to this fragility. Where Williams locates meaning in particular things, Oliver relocates it in a world that precedes and exceeds the individual altogether. Meaning is no longer dependent on memory, domestic intimacy, or the preservation of a certain kind of consciousness. It is available regardless of one’s personal history. The world goes on.

There is a type of transcendence in this: this meaning transcends the individual and is **always already there**.

## De-Centering Despair

The poem then makes a crucial shift:

> Tell me about your despair, yours, and I will tell you mine.
> Meanwhile the world goes on.

Despair is acknowledged, even shared—but it is decisively de-centred. This feels very different from [Eliot]({% post_url 2026-01-03-the-utility-of-tradition %}) or [Yeats]({% post_url 2026-01-04-the-architecture-of-meaning %}), where despair threatens to become cosmically significant. Oliver does not deny despair; she relativises it. Everyone has it. It does not define you. And it does not stop the world.

Nature continues regardless:

> Meanwhile the sun and the clear pebbles of the rain
> are moving across the landscapes…

There is something deeply consoling here, but also something quietly unsettling. Human concern is no longer the axis around which meaning turns.

This becomes clearest in the poem’s central image: the **wild geese**.

The geese are not symbolic in the Yeatsian sense, nor are they dependent on human attention in the way Williams’ objects sometimes are. They are wild—collective, directional, autonomous. Their meaning is not processed or shaped by us. They do not need to be interpreted in order to exist meaningfully. They simply move.

## The Family of Things

And yet Oliver turns back toward the reader:

> Whoever you are, no matter how lonely,
> the world offers itself to your imagination…
> announcing your place
> in the family of things.

This is where the poem becomes genuinely transcendent. It offers consolation without doctrine, inclusion without effort.

There is something unmistakably Emersonian here: the sense that meaning is already present, that the self can dissolve into something larger, and that the world offers itself freely once we stop obstructing it. It also resonates with ideas found in Zen and in Heidegger—the sense that what we seek is “always already there,” and that striving itself is often the barrier.

In this sense, *Wild Geese* feels like a resolution to the tension I’ve been circling between **immanence** and **transcendence**. Oliver incorporates both. Meaning is immediate and bodily, but also larger than the individual. It does not depend on attention, culture, or belief. The world goes on, and in doing so, it includes us.

## The Unease: Meaning Without Need

And yet, I find myself uneasy.

The meaning Oliver offers is vivid, generous, and alive—but it is also not **human-scaled**. It is not shaped around obligation, responsibility, or the slow work of sustaining a life. Wild geese do not build homes, preserve traditions, or carry concern forward. They move, they call, they pass through.

The meaning they embody is real, but **it does not need us**.

That may be precisely its power. But it also raises a difficult question. A meaning that does not need us can console us—but can it ground the fragile, human work of living? Can it sustain care, responsibility, and the creation of shared worlds? Or does it ultimately sidestep those demands by outscaling them?

Compared with Williams, whose meaning is intimate and domestic, Oliver’s meaning is vast and impersonal. Compared with Yeats, it is warm rather than cold. But it still leaves something unresolved. The consolation it offers is real, yet it comes at the cost of diminishing the significance of human-made meaning—of craft, inheritance, and concern.

## Conclusion

For now, *Wild Geese* feels like a genuine resting place. It reminds me that despair is not the measure of the world, that meaning exceeds us, and that life continues regardless of our inner turbulence.

But the question it leaves me with is not closed. If anything, it is growing.

What kind of meaning can both exceed us and remain answerable to human life?
What kind of meaning can console us without rendering our concerns incidental?

I don’t yet have an answer. But reading Oliver has sharpened the question—and that, in itself, feels like progress.
