Modern liberal democracies are masterpieces of horizontal engineering. Through free markets, civic engagement, and institutional checks and balances, they are incredibly effective at managing resources, resolving disputes procedurally, and minimizing visible friction across a decentralized network. 

But a highly optimized system can run flawlessly and still feel profoundly empty if it lacks a higher orientation. To its sharpest critics, this is liberalism's fatal structural flaw. 

Thinkers as different as Fyodor Dostoevsky and Friedrich Nietzsche looked at the emerging liberal order and diagnosed the exact same problem: it is horizontally brilliant, but vertically hollow. They argued that liberalism is incapable of providing **Moral Verticality**—a structural orientation toward something higher that justifies sacrifice, demands excellence, or gives profound meaning to human existence. It can regulate behavior, but it cannot orient the soul.

This criticism is incredibly powerful, but a systems-level diagnosis reveals something more complicated. It isn't that liberalism completely lacks a vertical axis; it’s that its verticality is an *undocumented dependency*. 

In the Anglosphere, liberalism runs on a hidden substrate of interiorised Protestantism and historical civic culture. It possesses a *de facto* moral verticality, but because this foundation is heavily obfuscated by modern procedural language, the system cannot easily defend or articulate its own roots. Dostoevsky and Nietzsche were pointing at a very real structural vulnerability. 

To see why liberalism appears vertically hollow, why it is so vulnerable to this critique, and why it actually relies on the very structures it claims to have outgrown, we have to look at how the West refactored its civilisational stack.

## I. Two Primitives: Jesus and Socrates

To understand the architecture of meaning that preceded liberalism, we can look at the two foundational primitives of the Western inheritance. The ultimate stress-test of any vertical orientation is how it processes the ultimate extreme: human suffering and death. 

Thomas More—executed by Henry VIII for refusing to accept the authority of the Church of England—wrote from the Tower of London about the difference between Jesus and Socrates. He notes that Jesus, in the Gospels, weeps openly—but is never described as laughing. Socrates, in Plato, is the opposite: he jokes, he teases, he remains composed—but never weeps.

Both figures are executed by their respective political systems. But the character of their deaths—and how their followers process that suffering—differs fundamentally.

Jesus is tortured in one of the most prolonged and painful forms of execution available. His suffering is explicit, central, and inseparable from the meaning of his death. Socrates, by contrast, is given hemlock—a comparatively quick and controlled end. His death is calm, almost philosophical. The emphasis is not on the suffering itself, but on the reasoning that leads to it, and the manner in which it is met.

These point toward two distinct ways of relating suffering to meaning:

1. **The Christian Protocol:** The death of Christ is not simply an event, but an orientation. It is a decisive rupture where the structure of history itself is altered. Suffering is no longer an anomaly to be endured; it becomes something that can be taken up and made redemptive in itself. The victim of suffering is ultimately identified as one and the same as God. René Girard would later argue this existential stance of the saviour of mankind makes scapegoating fundamentally difficult.
2. **The Socratic Protocol:** Plato’s *Apology* treats the execution of Socrates as a structural failure of the political architecture. The jury condemns him not because his reasoning is flawed, but because the structure of judgement itself (the *demos*) is misaligned with truth. Socrates does not redeem the suffering. He clarifies the conditions under which it occurs—and demonstrates how an individual can remain aligned with truth even when the surrounding system fails. 

## II. The Immanent Turn: Nietzsche’s Structural Audit

For Friedrich Nietzsche, both Christianity and the classical Greek philosophical tradition represent attempts to render suffering meaningful, but they do so by *spiritualizing* it. They do not confront suffering as a raw fact of existence. Instead, they reinterpret it—transforming weakness into a form of strength by relocating value into another register. Moral purity becomes power. Suffering becomes virtue. 

In doing so, these cultural moments introduced certain "primitives" into Western culture that eventually became massive architectural misalignments. Rather than orienting people toward *this* world, they direct attention toward a detached domain in which suffering is magically resolved (the World of Forms, or the Kingdom of Heaven). 

Nietzsche contrasts this with the Greek tragedians. In tragedy, suffering is neither explained away nor overcome. It is intrinsic to existence, faced without appeal to a higher resolution. The tragedians do not seek to escape suffering; they remain with it. 

For Nietzsche, the deferred resolutions of Platonism and Christianity are a profound misorientation. They encourage people to organize their lives around a structure that does not fully exist in reality. But in my opinion, Nietzsche does not simply reject these traditions—he tries to fulfill their underlying projects. 

He wants to keep the power of orientation and overcoming, but run it entirely locally. There is no other world. No deferred resolution. The highest orientation must be found *here*. Verticality doesn't require false beliefs in another world.

In some ways, this is an anti-metaphysical move, similar to Buddhism. But unlike Zen, which is fundamentally stateless and dissolves the striving ego to escape suffering, Nietzsche keeps verticality. He keeps future orientation. His view of the world is inherently cumulative: we are overcoming ourselves across Deep Time—we see this throughout the history of civilisation. Nietzsche bootstraps us into the correct layer of analysis. His "philosophising with a hammer" is an act of structural testing—sounding out foundations to see where they hold and where they are hollow. The aim is not collapse, but realignment.

## III. The Realist Refactor: Machiavelli and Hobbes

Dostoevsky and Nietzsche share a common enemy in rationalist moralism: both think pure reason cannot generate a binding highest good. Dostoevsky therefore defends Christianity as the indispensable, load-bearing source of moral order. Nietzsche attacks it as a life-denying inversion of natural hierarchy. 

In this respect, Nietzsche shares a deep affinity with Niccolò Machiavelli. 

Machiavelli’s scandal lies in his refusal to derive political architecture from a prior moral or metaphysical design. In a world still trying to run the synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology, he works backward from empirical reality. He looks at what actually succeeds under conditions of necessity, and redefines virtue (*virtù*) accordingly. 

He is not immoral; he is amoral. He simply subordinates the moral framework to the demands of system survival—in this sense, he runs on the [Discovery Principle]({% post_url 2026-03-04-the-discovery-principle %}). *The Prince* is not merely a mirror for rulers; it is a work that uses the genre ironically to expose the actual grammar of effective political life. On the surface, it instructs rulers; beneath the surface, it critiques them with heavy irony.

Thomas Hobbes enacts a comparable refactor. Without mounting a direct theological refutation, Hobbes and Machiavelli effectively dissolve the medieval "Great Chain of Being"—the idea that political authority derives its permissions directly from a cosmos ordered by God. Instead, they relocate political justification onto an immanent footing: legitimacy is evaluated purely by the system's capacity to produce stability and prevent the catastrophic failure of the "state of nature."

There is a striking irony in the Western tradition. Socrates is executed; Machiavelli is exiled; Hobbes writes in the shadow of civil war; Milton writes after the collapse of the English Commonwealth. In each case, their own direct political interventions failed. But the conceptual reframing of politics that followed proved far more enduring. They architected deeper shifts in the lens through which political life is understood, proving that true upstream shifts in ontology and epistemology can have incredible power downstream to reshape institutions.

## IV. The Procedural Turn: Anglo-French Liberalism

Many of these realist figures formed the intellectual backbone of the Anglo-French liberal tradition. And while liberalism is historically entangled with Protestantism, it represents a profound shift away from the underlying cultural grammar of Christianity.

In the Christian framework, morality is a vertical integration. It is grounded in transcendence. By contrast, liberalism increasingly reframes morality in procedural and institutional terms. Justice is no longer a cosmic property; it is something administered through courts, laws, and the state. This is why Hobbes is taken as a proto-liberal, despite his monarchism. Hobbes justifies monarchy in procedural and structural terms as necessary to maintain social order, rather than through theological or moral terms.

Another influence on the proceduralism of liberalism is the British common law tradition. Common law emerged as a negotiated synthesis: incoming Norman rulers overlaying their authority onto a preexisting fabric of Anglo-Saxon customs. Crucially, certain rights were treated as *prior* to formal legislation. They were not created by the state; they were recognized by it. Law emerged as an evolving negotiation.

When John Locke articulated the natural rights of life, liberty, and property, he was not inventing an abstract philosophy; he was formalizing an already existing legal and cultural intuition. He translated a historically embedded legal sensibility into a universal political theory.

### The "Fail Early" Architecture
From a systems perspective, the Anglosphere political tradition is implicitly oriented toward early failure and continuous error correction. It does not eliminate conflict; rather, it creates conditions in which conflict is surfaced, contested, and renegotiated relatively early. 

This explains a historical asymmetry: the English Civil War occurred a century before the American and French Revolutions. The subsequent Restoration of the monarchy might appear as a regression, but it was actually a structural update. The monarchy returned on altered terms, implicitly conditional, and embedded within a political culture that had already internalized limits on power. Authority could no longer rest on the same unquestioned foundations. 

This is precisely what struck Voltaire during his visit to Britain. He was impressed not merely by the presence of liberty, but by the mechanism: a system in which power is balanced, constrained, and rendered predictable through institutional design rather than anchored in a transcendent moral order.

> “The English are the only people upon earth who have been able to prescribe limits to the power of kings by resisting them; and who, by a series of struggles, have at last established that wise Government where the Prince is all-powerful to do good, and, at the same time, is restrained from committing evil; where the nobles are great without insolence, though there are no vassals; and where the people share in the Government without confusion.”  
> — *Letters on the English* by Voltaire

## V. The Crisis of Missing Dependencies

But liberalism creates a structural weakness. It emerges historically as a negotiated settlement between competing value systems. Its solution does not openly resolve these differences at the level of truth, but contains them procedurally.

This apparent neutrality is misleading. In the Anglosphere, liberalism is underwritten by a set of implicit cultural orientations: habits of restraint, respect for law, individual responsibility, and an ingrained valuation of freedom. It relies on a dense, pre-existing network of local dependencies. A culture which is fundamentally oriented towards civic engagement.

When liberalism is exported in its abstracted form to environments without that underlying substrate, it risks becoming a formal doctrine without a corresponding social reality. 

This tension was obvious to Fyodor Dostoevsky in 19th-century Russia. Liberal and rationalist ideas were being imported by the educated elite, but Dostoevsky saw that they lacked a compelling moral orientation. Liberalism could organise society procedurally, but it struggled to justify *why* one ought to suffer, sacrifice, or subordinate oneself to something higher. 

It appeared to lack **Moral Verticality**.

In the Christian tradition, suffering is rendered meaningful through alignment with a higher moral order. Liberalism, however, similarly to Plato, tends to treat suffering as a failure condition of the system—a bug to be minimized or engineered away (as seen in John Stuart Mill's utilitarianism). It seeks to reduce, manage, or redistribute suffering, but it does not redeem it.

### Universal Claims vs. Realist Backends
This dependency gap is glaring at the international level. Liberalism presents itself as a universal, self-sufficient moral system. Institutions like the League of Nations and the UN have sought to articulate a moral verticality against aggression and human rights violations. 

But in practice, liberal norms have always depended on an underlying realist architecture of power to sustain them. Rights require enforcement, and enforcement requires sovereign authority. This is the undocumented Hobbesianism and Machiavellianism that underlies liberalism in practice. The UN provides the API of international norms, but it lacks this backend to enforce moral vetoes against bad actors. Where power structures are absent or contested, liberal norms tend to fragment. When they are enforced by liberal nations, it appears inconsistent and hypocritical from the outside, since the enforcement mechanisms are not openly discussed or justified.

### The Hollowing of Conscience
At the individual level, without the undocumented substrate, the abstraction of liberalism hollows out the soul. However, as Max Weber argued in his analysis of the "Protestant work ethic," Reformed Protestantism actually internalized religion. Conscience became a demanding, costly obligation before God that directed moral energy into disciplined, worldly engagement. By this model, secular liberals have actually deeply internalised certain orientations from Protestantism, even though they no longer align with the supernatural beliefs.

As liberalism spreads elsewhere, this deeper structure is attenuated. The language of conscience remains, but the practices and orientation that ground it are interpreted as protected private preference. What was once an existential burden becomes a juridical category. The *right* to believe replaces the *obligation* to orient oneself toward truth. Freedom of belief is preserved, but detached from its formative moral structure. Liberalism in this form fails to transform individuals because the API doesn't contain the relevant software.

## VI. Nietzsche and the "Nation of Shopkeepers"

Friedrich Nietzsche likewise saw liberalism as a kind of “thin gruel” for civilisation. What he objects to is not liberalism’s failure to produce order or prosperity, but precisely its success in doing so at the expense of higher aspirations. By orienting society around economics, utility, and the minimization of suffering, liberalism optimizes for comfort and risk-aversion. It flattens hierarchy and suppresses the conditions required for greatness.

> "The spirit of respectable but mediocre Englishmen—I cite Darwin, John Stuart Mill, and Herbert Spencer—is successfully gaining pre-eminence in the middle regions of European taste... the English with their profoundly average quality have already once brought about a collective depression of the European spirit. What people call ‘modern ideas’... were English in origin... European nastiness, the plebeian quality of modern ideas, is the work of England."
> 
> — Friedrich Nietzsche, *Beyond Good and Evil*
{: .prompt-quote }

Nietzsche is operating in full polemical mode here, but the tension is real. Being singled out by Nietzsche is a backhanded recognition of historical decisiveness. He places English culture in a position of enormous civilisational significance—effectively crediting it with shaping modernity—only to then characterize it as mediocre and spiritually diminishing. 

Nietzsche recognizes the disciplined, empirical, and technical orientation of English thought as a powerful engine of modernity, while simultaneously judging it to be totally inadequate as a foundation for higher existential achievement.

## VII. Conclusion: The Undocumented Axis

And so, we arrive at the crux. 

For Nietzsche, modern liberal civilisation is defined by an absence of verticality: it lacks an orientation toward greatness, self-overcoming, and the cultivation of higher human types. 

For Dostoevsky, modernity lacks moral verticality: it lacks an orientation toward something beyond the self that can ground sacrifice and transform character. Where Nietzsche saw Christianity as a life-denying framework to be overcome, Dostoevsky saw it as the deep, existential truth of reality.

Both perceive modern liberal civilisation as critically incomplete. It is highly effective at managing coordination horizontally across institutions, but entirely deficient in its capacity to orient human beings vertically.

Both critiques are incredibly powerful, but they ultimately misdiagnose the mechanics of the liberal order. Liberalism does not run on empty procedures; it runs on a hidden substrate. In the Anglosphere, those base layers are essentially interiorised Protestantism, disciplined worldly engagement, and historic common law. The system *does* have a *de facto* moral verticality—one capable of driving world-historic system updates like the abolition of slavery. 

But because this verticality remains largely undocumented and implicit, it occupies an unstable, vulnerable position in our modern architecture. It cannot easily defend or justify itself in explicit terms. What appears on the surface as a value-neutral, procedural system actually depends on deep moral structures that it no longer fully articulates. 

And interrogating that hidden axis is exactly what we need to debug next.
