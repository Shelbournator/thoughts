As I argued in [Higher Education and the Compression Problem]({% post_url 2026-05-10-higher-education-and-the-compression-problem %}) and [From Paideia to Credentials]({% post_url 2026-05-11-from-paideia-to-credentials %}), most arguments about higher education fail before they begin because they compress the institution too early.

Compression is not always a mistake; it is often necessary. We need shorthand models in order to think, argue, compare, and decide. A slogan, a ranking, a credential, or a single metric can all be useful for some purposes. The problem begins when a compressed model is used for a task that requires a full architectural view.

If we are asking what value higher education can produce, for whom, by what mechanisms, and at what cost, shorthand is no longer enough. At that point, we need a way to separate the different forms of value the institution produces, how those values are generated, where they conflict, which parts are invisible from the outside, and which forms of value matter most to individuals and civilisation over time.

One person is arguing about employability. Another is arguing about research. Another is arguing about civic formation, social mobility, intellectual culture, professional competence, or status signalling. The disagreement often looks ideological, but underneath it is architectural: different people are treating different forms of value as if they were the whole.

The difficulty is that many of these forms of value are real. Employability matters. Research matters. Civic formation, intellectual culture, social mobility, personal development, and legitimacy also matter. But they do not all matter in the same way, to the same people, over the same timescale, or through the same mechanisms. Some value is private, some public; some economic, some civic; some immediate and measurable, some delayed, diffuse, and incredibly hard to attribute.

Higher education is therefore not trying to do one thing. It is trying to hold together multiple forms of value inside a single institutional form. That can be powerful: one reason universities have endured is that they bundle teaching, research, credentialling, socialisation, networks, and cultural transmission in ways that reinforce each other. But it also creates immense tension, because one organisation cannot mathematically maximise all of these values at once.

Different societies must choose different priorities. A society worried about innovation may emphasise research and technical capability. A society worried about civic fragmentation may emphasise shared culture, judgement, and citizenship. A society worried about productivity may emphasise employability. None of these choices is neutral, and none removes the trade-offs.

There is nothing wrong with evaluating one form of value narrowly. But once we name a value, we have to audit the system. Is the university actually producing that value? Is it producing it well? And is the university the best institutional form for producing it?

If the aim is to produce well-rounded human beings, we have to ask whether universities do that better than travel, work, friendship, responsibility, or ordinary life experience. If the aim is to produce innovative knowledge, we have to ask whether universities are the best environment for discovery, or whether some knowledge is better produced in independent institutes, startups, or by autodidacts. If the aim is to preserve civilisation's knowledge base, we have to ask whether universities are still maintaining, transmitting, and renewing that inheritance.

This is why the problem is architectural. Higher education is not one value-producing machine. It is a historically accumulated institution that produces many forms of value at once. The central question is therefore not which single value should dominate, but how to separate the forms of value clearly enough to ask better questions: what is being produced, for whom, by what mechanism, at what cost, and with what trade-offs?

## The Broader Functions of Higher Education

The first step is to open up the two broad categories of output: **people** and **knowledge**.

On the *people* side, universities do not only teach students. They credential them, filter them, socialise them, connect them to networks, prepare them for professions, expose them to intellectual cultures, and sometimes help form their judgement, character, independence, and sense of vocation.

On the *knowledge* side, universities do not only produce research. They preserve knowledge, transmit it, interpret it, criticise it, validate it, apply it, and make it available to wider publics. Some of this work produces new discoveries; some of it maintains the conditions under which discovery remains possible.

The important point is not that every university performs all of these functions equally. It is that many different functions are being carried by the exact same institutional hardware.

In the contemporary system, some of these functions are much more visible than others. Credentials, labour-market signals, professional preparation, research output, grant income, publications, and institutional prestige are highly legible. They can be compared, priced, audited, and exchanged.

Other functions are still present, but unevenly. Some universities still provide a serious liberal education: the cultivation of judgement, intellectual breadth, moral seriousness, and civic imagination. Some environments support deep personal formation: independence, friendship, taste, self-knowledge, and existential development. Some preserve traditions and forms of inquiry whose value may not be immediately visible. But these functions are less explicitly measured, less consistently delivered, and highly dependent on local subcommunities and the agency of particular individuals.

This matters because arguments about higher education quietly move between these functions. A defence of universities as engines of research does not automatically defend their credentialling role. A critique of degrees as weak labour-market signals does not imply universities are bad at producing knowledge. Once the functions are separated, the institution becomes easier to debug.

It also allows us to ask whether the university is always the best setting for each function. Some forms of existential development may be better served by travelling, taking on real responsibility, or moving through unfamiliar environments. Some forms of learning may be better served by apprenticeship or autodidactic immersion. Separating the functions does not prove universities are unnecessary; it simply stops us from assuming that every valuable function must be bundled into the same institutional form.

## Why Research and Credentials Become Dominant

The reason research and credentials dominate the modern university is not that they are its only real functions. It is that they are the functions most easily translated into **legible value**.

Legibility means that a form of value can be "seen" by institutions that are not close to the underlying activity. It can be counted, compared, audited, priced, ranked, or funded. Legibility is not the same as importance. It is merely a property of how easily value travels through large systems.

A university may increase a student’s earnings, deepen their judgement, preserve a tradition, or raise the prestige of a nation. These forms of value are all real, but they are not equally visible, immediate, or easy to price.

Foundational work is fundamentally illegible at the point of production. Its value may only become visible years later, when a person faces uncertainty, exercises judgement, leads under pressure, or applies a deep framework in a situation no syllabus could have anticipated.

This connects back to [Aristotle’s distinction]({% post_url 2026-05-11-from-paideia-to-credentials %}) between different kinds of knowledge. Outputs closer to *epistēmē* (demonstrable knowledge) or *poiēsis* (production) can be inspected, counted, or compared. Others are closer to *phronēsis*: practical judgement exercised in concrete situations under uncertainty. Modern institutions are much better at measuring the former than the latter. But the fact that *phronēsis* is harder to certify does not make it less valuable; in open, complex environments, it is precisely the capacity that matters most.

This is one reason the older ideals of liberal education and *Bildung* should not be dismissed too quickly. The ability to think for oneself under conditions of uncertainty may be hard to measure, but it is deeply valuable. The same is true of intellectual breadth, moral seriousness, courage, and character. These capacities may not map cleanly onto a job description, but they shape how someone acts when the environment becomes ambiguous, novel, or difficult.

Research is more legible because it translates easily into institutional and economic status. Publications, grants, citations, and patents allow knowledge production to be counted, compared, and funded. These metrics are imperfect and often distort the activity they measure, but they make research visible to governments and administrators.

Credentials are more legible because they attach a portable economic value to a person. A degree compresses a complex educational history into a simple marker that employers and immigration systems can process. It does not fully describe capability, judgement, or maturity, but it gives institutions something they can measure.

The result is a hard shift in institutional optimization. Universities still speak the inherited language of wisdom, culture, and public good, but their incentive structures ruthlessly reward the forms of value that can be most easily measured and exchanged. Over time, measurable proxies become the functions around which the system organises itself.

This does not require a conspiracy. It is a predictable effect of scale, bureaucracy, markets, and state funding. Large institutions need proxies to allocate money and justify themselves. The danger is not the use of proxies. The danger is forgetting what the proxy was meant to stand in for.

## Economic Value Is Not One Thing

Economic value is not a secondary or illegitimate concern. Higher education is expensive, time-consuming, publicly subsidised, and deeply connected to national capacity. It is entirely reasonable to ask what economic value it produces.

But economic value is not monolithic. On the "people" side, the economic value of a university is generated through four distinct mechanisms:

*   **Learning:** the university actually increases capability—knowledge, skill, judgement, discipline, adaptability, and practical competence.
*   **Filtering:** the university selects for people who already had valuable traits, such as intelligence, conscientiousness, persistence, or prior educational advantage.
*   **Signalling:** the degree gives those traits a portable marker that employers can recognise.
*   **Allocation:** the system helps route people into jobs, firms, networks, and opportunities where their capabilities can be used.

These mechanisms are often bundled together, but they are not the same. If universities mainly create value through *learning*, then the economic question is whether the increase in capability justifies the cost. If they mainly create value through *filtering and signalling*, then the question is whether society is using an extremely expensive sorting mechanism. Economists such as Bryan Caplan have pressed this argument sharply: if much of education functions as signalling rather than skill formation, then the private return to a degree may remain high even when the social return is much weaker.

This does not prove that university is “just signalling.” A degree may indicate intelligence, conformity, social class, institutional navigation, domain knowledge, or genuine capability. It does not distinguish these cleanly.

That matters for everyone involved. Students may optimise for brand and employability signals rather than capability. Universities may optimise for graduate outcomes and rankings rather than deep learning. Employers may outsource selection to universities instead of developing better ways to assess capability. Universities themselves should care about this, because if credentials from their institution cease to signal real value, they slowly undermine one of their own core functions.

The effects may not be merely inefficient, but formative. If the system rewards conformity, risk-aversion, institutional navigation, and optimisation for legible approval, then it may teach some of the most capable young people to become better at those behaviours. Instead of cultivating independent judgement, courage, or creativity, it may select for and reinforce the exact traits that succeed inside credentialled bureaucratic environments.

> "Our educational system both drives and reflects our obsession with competition. Grades themselves allow precise measurement of each student’s competitiveness; pupils with the highest marks receive status and credentials. We teach every young person the same subjects in mostly the same ways, irrespective of individual talents and preferences. Students who don’t learn best by sitting still at a desk are made to feel somehow inferior, while children who excel on conventional measures like tests and assignments end up defining their identities in terms of this weirdly contrived academic parallel reality.
>
> And it gets worse as students ascend to higher levels of the tournament. Elite students climb confidently until they reach a level of competition sufficiently intense to beat their dreams out of them. Higher education is the place where people who had big plans in high school get stuck in fierce rivalries with equally smart peers over conventional careers like management consulting and investment banking. For the privilege of being turned into conformists, students (or their families) pay hundreds of thousands of dollars in skyrocketing tuition that continues to outpace inflation."
> 
> — Peter Thiel, *Zero to One*

On the "knowledge" side, the underlying economic value is not research output as such, but useful knowledge, innovation, technical capability, and the expansion of what societies are able to do. Research metrics can help identify some of this value, but they can also become weak proxies. Publications and citations may reward volume, fashion, safe incrementalism, and work legible to existing disciplinary incentives. Useful, foundational, or long-horizon work may be harder to recognise. Historically, some of the most foundational work that went on to establish whole disciplines and industries has often been in areas considered unimportant or uninteresting by mainstream research.

This matters because a system can become economically oriented in appearance while remaining poorly aligned with economic value in substance. It can produce more credentials without producing more capability. It can produce more publications without producing more useful knowledge. Separately from any questions about whether universities should be primarily oriented towards economic utility—they often are, and often should be—the question is which mechanism is producing the value, how efficiently it is doing so, and whether the visible markers are still connected to the capabilities they are supposed to represent.

## Frontier Technology as a Test Case

Frontier technology is a useful test case because it is one of the places where formal credentials are most visibly subordinated to demonstrated capability. This is not because technology is uniquely anti-educational, but because the structure of the work exposes the gap between the credential and capability.

1.  **Tight Feedback Loops:** In many institutional environments, status, fluency, credentials, and conformity can substitute for performance for a long time. In frontier technology, especially software, the feedback loops are shorter. Code runs or it does not. Systems scale or they do not. Users adopt them or they do not. This makes some forms of capability harder to fake indefinitely.
2.  **Market Competition:** In elite professions, entry is tightly mediated by licensing and institutional reputation. In technology, highly competent people can build their own product and compete with established players directly. Capability can become immediate market pressure.
3.  **The Scale of Returns:** The pipeline of highly competent technical people is limited, and winners can win very big. A single exceptional engineer, founder, or product thinker can change the trajectory of a company, generating billions in value. This changes the hiring logic. If exceptional capability is scarce and enormously valuable, filtering too heavily by credentials becomes an unacceptable operational risk: it excludes the exact outlier talent the system most needs to find.
4.  **Holistic Competence:** Building valuable technology is not only a matter of possessing demonstrable knowledge. It requires judgement, taste, speed of learning, product sense, social perception, and the ability to act under uncertainty. These capacities overlap with formal education, but they are incredibly difficult to compress into a credential.

This makes the university relationship highly ambiguous. Some prospective students are already operating near the frontier before the institution can add much direct educational value. Their education is happening elsewhere: through building, open-source work, online communities, technical documentation, and direct exposure to problems. In such cases, university may provide a network, status, and optionality, but it can also impose a massive opportunity cost by pulling them into standardised pathways when they are already learning faster through contact with the frontier itself.

Stanford makes this pattern especially visible. Stanford has been one of the most important institutions in the history of technology precisely because it concentrates talent, status, networks, capital, technical culture, and proximity to live problems. 

Many of Stanford's most famous technology stories show this ambiguity. Larry Page and Sergey Brin developed the early Google project while graduate students at Stanford. Jerry Yang and David Filo built what became Yahoo while Stanford graduate students before co-founding the company. Evan Spiegel and Bobby Murphy met at Stanford before building Snapchat. Sam Altman left Stanford to build Loopt and later returned to teach start-up formation. In these cases, the university is not absent, but it is not simply functioning as a normal instructional pipeline. It is functioning as a talent cluster, network, legitimacy layer, and launchpad.

This helps explain why scepticism toward credentials is not confined to a few contrarian founders. Peter Thiel and Elon Musk are among the more explicit and rhetorically forceful examples, but the pattern is broader. Google, one of the most selective and data-driven technology companies, famously reduced its reliance on GPAs, test scores, and transcripts after finding that these were weak predictors of performance beyond the earliest career stage. The critique of credentials emerged directly from operational hiring data.

Bill Gates illustrates a different version of the same pattern. He has argued that, for most people, completing a degree is a much surer path to success. But his own trajectory still matters. Gates left Harvard not because he rejected education, but because he was already building in a field where capability could be demonstrated directly, and where the opportunity cost of remaining inside the institution was unusually high. Like many technology figures, he remained deeply educated in a broader sense: through reading, building, conversation, and self-directed learning.

Other examples point toward the breadth of education that technology can reward. Reid Hoffman (LinkedIn) and Stewart Butterfield (Slack) have both connected their philosophical training to entrepreneurship and leadership. Patrick and John Collison (Stripe) can be read as part of a broader pattern of technological entrepreneurship joined to intellectual curiosity, progress-oriented thinking, and cross-domain reading. These examples are not arguments *against* education. They are evidence that the forms of education most valuable at the frontier often exceed what the credential itself can certify.

Frontier environments create selection pressure for capacities that resemble older educational ideals: [Bildung]({% post_url 2026-05-11-from-paideia-to-credentials %}) as intellectual autonomy, classics and humanism as civilisational thinking, and liberal education as the cultivation of judgement under uncertainty.

One reason *Bildung* matters is that technically specific skills can often be acquired later by people with strong intellectual autonomy and self-directed learning capacity. Humboldt made this point directly over two centuries ago:

> "There are undeniably certain kinds of knowledge that must be of a general nature and, more importantly, a certain cultivation of the mind and character that nobody can afford to be without. People obviously cannot be good craftworkers, merchants, soldiers or businessmen unless, regardless of their occupation, they are good, upstanding and – according to their condition – well-informed human beings and citizens. If this basis is laid through schooling, vocational skills are easily acquired later on, and a person is always free to move from one occupation to another, as so often happens in life."

The reverse is less reliable: narrow technical training does not necessarily produce independence of mind. This is why frontier environments may select for people who can learn new domains quickly rather than people who merely possess the currently legible credential. 

Historically, universities aimed to produce people capable of judgement, leadership, and independent thought. In the modern system, some of the highest-leverage environments select for those capacities outside or against the credential pipeline. The most economically valuable actors are not always those who have accumulated the most legible credentials, but those who can construct new frames when existing ones stop working.

## What Capabilities Can Education Actually Certify?

This brings us to one of the most practical questions in the whole architecture: what capabilities can reasonably be inferred from someone’s education? 

Certification is fundamentally a lossy compression problem. A person’s education involves years of learning, practice, socialisation, assessment, institutional navigation, and personal development. A credential compresses that complex history into a portable signal. This is useful, because employers, governments, and professional bodies need ways to make decisions under uncertainty. But the compression loses vast amounts of fidelity.

Some inferences are relatively reasonable. A degree may indicate exposure to a body of knowledge, completion of a structured programme, capacity to meet deadlines, ability to navigate institutions, and a willingness to persist through externally imposed requirements. In more technical fields, it may indicate regulated domain knowledge.

Other inferences are much more difficult. Judgement, creativity, courage, intellectual independence, practical competence, moral seriousness, leadership, and the ability to act well under uncertainty are incredibly hard to certify directly. They may develop during university, but they are not easily captured by the credential itself.

There is a further complication: **the institution best placed to certify a form of value is not always the institution best placed to produce it.** 

A university may be able to certify certain kinds of knowledge, persistence, and institutional navigation better than it produces them. Meanwhile, other environments may produce valuable capabilities more powerfully, but certify them less cleanly. Travel, work, entrepreneurship, military service, serious friendship, civic participation, and autodidactic immersion may all form parts of the person that matter deeply. But they do not always produce portable, standardized signals that large institutions can easily recognise.

This is one reason universities remain powerful even when some of their functions could, in principle, be performed elsewhere. They do not merely produce value; they make some forms of value *legible*. But legibility should not be confused with production. 

This matters for students and parents because it clarifies what higher education can and cannot be expected to provide. A degree may be valuable as a credential, a learning environment, a professional pathway, a social network, or a setting for personal development. But those are different forms of value, and they are not guaranteed by the same mechanism.

It matters for employers because educational credentials should be interpreted as partial signals. A degree may help identify candidates worth considering, but it does not remove the need to assess the capabilities required by the actual work: skill, judgement, problem-solving, reliability, and fit.

And it matters for universities because certification affects incentives. Whatever an institution certifies becomes something students and departments can optimise toward. Universities should be highly concerned that credentials from their institution actually signal real value. If the credential becomes detached from meaningful capability, knowledge, or judgement, the institution slowly undermines its own core function.

## Why Reform Is Hard

Once the value architecture is separated in this way, reform becomes both more necessary and more difficult. It is no longer enough to say that higher education should be cheaper, more useful, more rigorous, more humane, or more innovative. Each of those aims points toward a different, competing part of the bundle.

This is why conversations about education reform are so often frustrating. The debate is not really two-sided; it is a high-dimensional space of partially overlapping compressions. Some defend the existing system within the economic frame, emphasising access, employability, research output, and institutional scale. Others critique the system within that exact same frame, pointing to credential inflation, weak labour-market signals, or bad ROI.

Others reject the economic frame entirely. They want universities to form citizens capable of thinking for themselves, emphasizing wisdom, character, intellectual independence, and spiritual seriousness. Often, each position is pointing at something real. The conversation becomes confused because these arguments are not only disagreeing about performance; they are disagreeing about what the objective function of higher education actually is.

Even when a diagnosis is right, improving the system is a separate problem. A reform may improve one outcome while damaging another. It may succeed locally but fail when scaled. It may produce short-term measurable gains while weakening less visible forms of value.

This is why reform movements often fail when they redesign around a narrow compression. If the problem is credential inflation, they try to replace degrees with skills tests. If the problem is poor teaching, they try to optimise instruction. If the problem is a lack of employability, they make education highly vocational. Each response captures something real, but risks losing functions that were being carried less visibly by the existing system.

The difficulty is partly human: whole-person education is not a simple production process. People differ in temperament, attention, motivation, and prior preparation. The same institution can be transformative for one student, merely credentialling for another, and actively deforming for a third.

The difficulty is also organisational. Universities are old, layered institutions with massive technical debt: departments, disciplines, professional guilds, funding models, status hierarchies, and alumni networks. Changing one part of the system changes incentives everywhere. 

And the difficulty is epistemic. Many of the things we care about most—judgement, intellectual independence, moral seriousness, courage, and practical wisdom—are hard to measure. If reformers measure only what is easy to measure, they may simply reproduce the exact same proxy problem in a new form.

Reform has to begin with a clearer model of the problem space. Before asking *how* higher education should change, we need to ask *what problems* it is supposed to solve: which functions it currently serves, which should be preserved, which should be separated, and which trade-offs we are willing to accept.

## Do Universities Need To Do Everything?

Once the different forms of value are separated clearly, we can ask a more architectural question: do they all need to be produced by the same monolithic institution? Could some be produced better, more affordably, or more efficiently through other structures? 

At present, many forms of value are bundled together partly because the system is inherited, partly because we do not usually think architecturally about education, and partly because legitimacy creates collective action problems. Even if another pathway could produce capability more efficiently, it may struggle to be trusted or recognized at scale.

**Learning value** is already partly unbundled. MOOCs, bootcamps, professional certificates, apprenticeships, and independent study can sometimes teach specific skills more directly than a university course. For motivated learners, these routes are extraordinarily powerful. But they often lack the surrounding architecture that makes learning socially legible: trusted standards, peer comparison, mentorship, and a recognized credential.

**Research value** can also be separated from teaching, at least in principle. Dedicated research institutes, think tanks, corporate research groups, and independent scholars can produce knowledge outside universities, often with fewer teaching obligations. Indeed, some of the most foundational ideas have come from outsiders working partly outside formal structures, precisely because they are less constrained by inherited academic frames. But separating research from teaching removes students from contact with people working at the frontier of knowledge. The Humboldtian argument remains strong: students should not only receive settled knowledge, but encounter inquiry while it is still alive and contested.

**Professional value** can be separated too. Some professions require structured training, supervised practice, licensing, or apprenticeship more than abstract study. But as higher education has increasingly been used as a platform for opportunity allocation, more professions have erected degree-based gatekeeping mechanisms. This can expand access in some respects, but it can also turn university into an artificial bottleneck for roles that were once entered through experience or employer training.

**Existential development** is perhaps the least obviously university-specific function, but it is one of the reasons university remains so powerful. Leaving home, forming friendships, encountering difference, managing freedom, testing identity, experiencing failure, and developing confidence can happen anywhere. For a fraction of the cost of university, travel or work can force a person to navigate risk and responsibility in a way that is profoundly formative. But universities bundle this existential transition with intellectual work, social density, recognized status, and a protected environment.

**Civic and liberal value** may also be produced elsewhere, but not automatically. Reading groups, religious communities, intellectual societies, and serious friendships can all cultivate judgement and moral seriousness. But universities have historically been one of the few institutions with the scale, time, status, and curriculum to make this kind of cultivation a recognized social project. Many of the deepest sources of liberal education are profoundly alien. A reader who simply picks up an ancient book may gain something real, but without mediated guidance, they are likely to miss much of the civilisational conversation.

There are therefore good reasons these forms of value have historically been bundled together. Thick institutions coordinate things that are incredibly difficult to assemble separately: teachers, peers, curriculum, legitimacy, social density, public trust, and a recognized transition into adult life. A credential is often more valuable when it is attached to a real community with history. Liberal education is strengthened by proximity to research. Professional preparation benefits from intellectual development. 

This does not mean the existing bundle is automatically justified. It means we should stop treating it as either self-evidently necessary or self-evidently obsolete. A clearer model lets us ask which forms of value belong together, which could be produced elsewhere, which depend entirely on institutional legitimacy, and which students are actually being served by which parts of the bundle.

## Conclusion

The historical view shows us that higher education has rarely been one thing. It has preserved knowledge, transmitted traditions, formed elites, prepared citizens, trained professionals, produced research, allocated status, created networks, certified competence, supported personal development, and generated economic value. The confusion begins when one of these functions is treated as the whole.

This is why the compression problem matters. “Higher education is for learning” is true, but too vague. “Higher education is for jobs” is true in part, but too narrow. “Higher education is for research” captures one of the dominant modern outputs, but not the full institution. “Higher education is credentialling” explains much of the current incentive structure, but not everything universities have done or still can do.

At the broadest level, universities produce people and knowledge. But modern society rewards some forms of value more clearly than others. Research and credentials dominate because they are comparatively legible: they can be counted, ranked, funded, exchanged, and attached to economic or status value. Other forms of value—judgement, intellectual independence, civic imagination, moral seriousness, practical wisdom, existential maturity, and the capacity to act under uncertainty—may be just as real, but harder to measure, price, and certify.

The answer is therefore not simple nostalgia for an older model, simple disruption of the existing system, or simple optimisation around economic proxies. Each of those compressions sees something real and misses something important. The better move is architectural: separate the forms of value, understand the trade-offs, and ask which parts of the existing bundle should be preserved, improved, separated, or routed elsewhere.

Within the right architectural frame, there is a place for everything and everything is in its right place. The task is not to reduce higher education to one purpose, but to understand the incredible array of purposes it has accumulated, the value each can produce, and the conditions under which each is best served.
